A-iButler: AI Chatbot
for Vehicle Diagnostics

A conversational AI triage assistant that helps drivers quickly assess car symptoms, determine urgency, and decide on the right next steps. It was built using the Anthropic Claude API and the Stanford Design Thinking framework.

Context
Problem & Solution
⚠ The Problem

Most drivers don't know whether a car symptom like a grinding noise, a warning light, smoke under the hood; is an emergency or something they can monitor. This uncertainty leads to either expensive panic visits to mechanics, or dangerous delays when action is truly needed.

✓ The Solution

A-iButler is a conversational AI assistant that guides users through a structured diagnostic conversation, classifying symptoms into clear urgency levels (Urgent / Soon / Monitor) and recommending specific next steps — all without replacing a qualified mechanic.

5
Reflection
Key Insights
🎯
Prompt Engineering is the Core Product
The system prompt defining persona, scope boundaries, urgency rules, and output structure; is what transforms a general LLM into a reliable specialist tool. Getting the guardrails right mattered more than the UI.
🔒
Guardrails Are a Feature, Not a Limitation
Explicitly instructing the model never to give cost estimates, never to guarantee safety, and to always redirect off-topic queries builds user trust. Safety boundaries are part of good AI product design.
🧠
Design Thinking Shapes Better AI
Starting with empathy, truly understanding what an anxious, non-expert user needs produced a warmer, more reassuring conversational tone than a technically-focused approach would have. Human-centered design changes the output.
⚙️
API Architecture Has Real Constraints
Direct browser-to-API calls require CORS headers and a user-provided key. This constraint shaped the entire UX; a setup screen, in-memory key storage, and clear instructions. Technical constraints drive design decisions.
🌐
Narrow Scope = High Reliability
By constraining A-iButler strictly to vehicle diagnostics and testing it against off-topic queries ("Who won the Super Bowl?"), I confirmed that a well-scoped AI assistant is significantly more trustworthy and useful than a general-purpose one. Specialization is a design choice with real consequences.

What This Demonstrates
From Concept to Working AI Product

A-iButler demonstrates my ability to take a real human problem, the anxiety of not knowing how serious a car issue is, and translate it into a production-ready AI solution using industry-standard tools and a structured design methodology. This project shows not just technical implementation, but product thinking: defining scope deliberately, designing for a specific user persona, and building in safety guardrails that make the tool trustworthy.

It represents the full lifecycle of AI product development: empathize → define → prototype → test → deploy — the same cycle I will bring to AI engineering roles in automotive systems, industrial control, and beyond.
